Identifying the genetic causes of resistance to anthelmintic compounds

Our understanding of the evolution of anthelmintic resistance (AR) in parasitic nematodes and the complex interactions between host and drug is limited. This limitation hinders our ability to predict, prevent, and slow the rate of AR worldwide. As an evolutionary biologist and quantitative geneticist, I aim to identify the causal mechanisms of resistance (MoR) to anthelmintic drugs and develop strategies to better combat neglected tropical diseases (NTDs) caused by parasitic nematodes.

About

I am a Post-doctoral Fellow at Johns Hopkins University in the laboratory of Dr. Erik Andersen. I earned my Ph.D. from the University of Georgia (UGA) in the laboratory of Dr. Arthur Edison, where I worked on an interdisciplinary consortium project and pursued research topics at the interface of chemistry, biochemistry, genetics, and statistical analysis. At UGA, I developed novel methods to identify unknown metabolites in the model nematode Caenorhabditis elegans. Critically, my graduate work revealed key insights into using C. elegans as a model to understand how genetics and environment affect metabolism, a skill that I am using to understand anthelmintic resistance in nematodes. To explore using C. elegans as a model to identify the MoR to anthelmintic drugs, I joined Dr. Erik Andersen’s laboratory. As an NIH NIAID F32 post-doctoral fellow, I learned quantitative genetics methods to identify the mechanisms of anthelmintic drug resistance using C. elegans and completed three major projects where I investigated: (1) how natural genetic diversity shapes nematode responses to anthelmintic compounds, (2) the repeated evolution of anthelmintic resistance across multiple Caenorhabditis species, and (3) the importance of studying multiple traits to understand how anthelmintic drugs affect nematode fitness. .
